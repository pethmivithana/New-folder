# Research Problem Statement

Mid-sprint requirement changes represent one of the most significant challenges in Agile software development, occurring in approximately 60-70% of software projects and contributing to schedule overruns, quality degradation, and team burnout. When new requirements or modifications to existing requirements emerge during an active sprint, project managers and development teams face a critical decision point: should the new requirement be added to the current sprint, deferred to a future sprint, split between sprints, or should it trigger a swap with an existing committed item? Current industry tools such as Jira, Azure DevOps, and Monday.com provide basic requirement tracking and sprint management capabilities, but lack sophisticated decision support mechanisms to analyze the multidimensional impact of requirement changes on sprint viability. These tools typically present only surface-level information about sprint capacity utilization (usually as a simple percentage of story points), without deeper analysis of how a new requirement affects effort estimation, schedule adherence risk, quality outcomes, and team productivity. This limitation forces teams to rely on manual heuristics, subjective judgment, and past experience to make critical decisions that directly impact project success, introducing inconsistency, suboptimal choices, and wasted effort in the decision-making process.

The core research problem emerges from the convergence of three fundamental gaps in current Agile tooling and practices. First, there is an absence of integrated, data-driven impact prediction that combines multiple dimensions of analysis—effort forecasting, schedule risk assessment, quality impact estimation, and productivity analysis—into a unified assessment framework. Second, teams lack automated mechanisms to verify whether incoming requirements align with the current sprint goal and strategic objectives, despite alignment being a critical factor in decision quality. Third, and most critically, there is no intelligent decision support engine that can synthesize the multidimensional impact analysis with alignment information and organizational constraints to recommend optimal actions with clear reasoning, leaving teams to navigate complex trade-offs without systematic guidance. The stakes of this problem are substantial: poor requirement change decisions directly cascade into project overruns, quality compromises, team morale degradation, and budget overruns that threaten project viability and organizational competitiveness.

Therefore, the overarching research problem addressed by this thesis is: **How can a comprehensive, intelligent system integrate machine learning-based impact prediction (effort, schedule risk, quality, and productivity), semantic alignment verification, rule-based decision logic, and real-time capacity tracking to provide teams with accurate, explainable recommendations for handling mid-sprint requirement changes, thereby improving sprint goal achievement, reducing project schedule variance, and enhancing team productivity?** This research problem encompasses the design and validation of four interconnected components—the Impact Prediction Module (combining ensemble ML models for multidimensional forecasting), the Sprint Goal Alignment Module (using NLP and semantic similarity for requirement-goal verification), the Decision Engine (implementing sophisticated rule-based logic for recommendation generation), and the Capacity Management Module (providing dynamic capacity calculation and 80% safety limit enforcement)—integrated into a unified decision support platform that teams can use in real-time during sprint execution to make more informed, consistent, and effective requirement change decisions.
